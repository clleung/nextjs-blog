Assistant in Gmail [Case Study]

Designer.

Role: Designer.

Team: Steven Orchosky, Rong Kang Chew, Emma Wang, Dian Lee.

Deliverables: App Analysis, User Scenarios, Adaptive User Interface (AUI) Opportunity Design, Figma Prototype.

Tools: Figma, Adaptive User Interface (AUI) Opportunities, Design Patterns, Product-Service Design.

Timeline: March - April 2021.

Location: Remote.

Overview

From learning Adaptive User Interfaces (AUI), our brief was to find an AUI opportunity within a mobile application that would fall under “low-hanging fruit”—a solution relatively easy to implement with AI that would maximize returns for the business and end-user.

We designed Assistant in Gmail, an AUI feature in Gmail that leverages event and guest data from Google Calendar to contextualize user access to emails. As a just-in-time feature, end-users would be able to find emails relevant to events—along with attached documents—quickly and have a more cohesive experience across the entirety of Google Workspace.

Goals

Find an Adaptive User Interface (AUI) Opportunity that is:

  1. Relatively easy to develop and provides a high return on investment for the business and end-users.

  2. Quantifiably improves end-user experience.

  3. Leverages design patterns to make the opportunity homogenous with the application.

Research

Application Analysis • Scenarios of Use

Application Analysis

To understand areas of opportunity, we analyzed 10 mobile applications to view opportunities for AUI.

To measure the potential impact of each opportunity, we indicated steps needed for

  1. The original path for the action.

  2. Where the user experience could be abbreviated from the baseline.

  3. The projected AUI path (which would use machine learning (ML) in some small way).


I analyzed Slack and Gmail, focusing on access and auto-prioritization of urgent emails.


Scenarios of Use

For each application, we also provided two scenarios to quickly understand the perspective of the user, as user research was not a factor in this project.

Professor Mellon often gets many emails from fellow faculty, administration, and students. They’re expecting emails from students about regrades, and would like to address them as soon as possible. With auto-prioritization, Gmail is able to mark down emails that need to be responded immediately by using data from commonly emailed individuals, individuals within the organization (and class, if Google Classroom is used) and the text of the email. As a result, Professor Mellon is able to meet the needs of his students more quickly.

Ideate

Potential AUI Opportunities • Critique

Potential AUI Opportunities

After selecting Gmail as our desired application, we ideated on potential applications, as a critique with our classmates and professors revealed that automatic email prioritization could result in detrimental inference errors.

To better leverage the power of AUI, we considered the following ideas:

  • Pre-archiving unwanted emails.

  • Using ranked lists to prioritize emails.

  • Auto-switching accounts.

  • Auto-composing emails.

  • Suggesting important emails to read.

  • Proactive filtering.

Critique

However, from further critique, we found that most of these ideas would interfere with current user flows and deviated from convention in other email applications, potentially confusing end-users’ mental model of the application’s base function.

Design

Pivot to Assistant in Gmail • Value Flow

Pivot to Assistant in Gmail

At this point of our project, we decided to make a major pivot to our discussion of AUI opportunities. We struggled to design for an opportunity that would justify the investment needed to develop the feature and account for potential inference errors.


Rather than trying to add a complete feature or an involuntary change to the user experience (such as pre-archiving or automation), we would introduce a voluntary intervention that could still provide the opportunity for time-savings.


Then, we wondered—why limit the data used by the AUI to only the data communicated in Gmail?

Value Flow

We expanded our concept to include Google Workspace, specifically Google Calendar, to show emails relevant to an event: a just-in-time (emails appear just before and during the event) or meeting preparation feature (emails appear during the event).

With event data (time, event name, attendees) and email data (sender, recipient, contents) available and secure within Google Workspace, we posited high returns for Google in the form of competitive advantage for enterprise-level Google Workspace (and higher revenue) along with labeled user data and user satisfaction.

From the end-user’s perspective, we considered the widespread benefits of saved time and reduced frustration, particularly in long engagements with team members, clients, or key stakeholders. Moreover, for enterprise users or users that have a third-party managing or adding meetings, it affords more streamlined communication between Gmail and Google Calendar.

Iterate

Just in Time AUI Opportunity • Design Patterns

Technology Solution

After considering potential solutions that would meet the pain points we discovered, our team decided to recommend a new workflow using Google Workspace (specifically for non-profits).

We found that Google Workspace not only had the ability to be HIPAA compliant (as of November 2021), but also required relatively little technology maintenance and had a minimal cost.

We decided against industry standards for data storage such as Amazon Web Services due to the lack of IT personnel in the organization and the time it would take to implement it in CAPSEA.

New Data Process

From our technology solution, we considered a new workflow that would meet the stakeholder needs.

In particular, we focused on redesigning internal processes for handling callers, using Google Forms, Google Docs, Google Sheets, and Gmail to digitize CAPSEA’s data collection and communication of information when necessary.

Deliver

Supporting Documents • Recommendation • Presentation

Provided our client with documentation of our recommendation to facilitate change within CAPSEA.

Supporting Documents

To share our insights and recommendations to CAPSEA in-depth, we created a business plan, training manual, and budget to:

  • Provide advice (from our research and SME interviews) for how to manage the transition in technology.

  • Display measurable, achievable, and actionable steps for implementing the technology solution and beginning training.

  • Reinforce the need for our recommendation and justify the cost.

Recommendation

For reference, our initial project goals:

  1. Determine the most appropriate data platform tailored for CAPSEA’s needs including confidentiality, accuracy, and affordability.
  2. Improve current process of accepting and processing clients.
  3. Generate a business plan, training manual, and budget for plan implementation.
Our recommendation—the use of Google Workspace and a new work process flow—met our initial project goals.
  • Google Workspace had HIPAA compliance capabilities, allowed for detailed data collection in Google Forms, and was affordable for CAPSEA.

  • Our new workflow would digitize CAPSEA’s operations for receiving calls and allow easier dissemination of information when needed between departments.

  • Our business plan, training manual, and budget (as well as prior reports) covered our process and final recommendation in-depth.

Presentation

After handing off our documentation and holding a final meeting with our client, we presented our process, findings, and final recommendation to over 30 individuals.

Next Steps

Reflection • Outcomes

Reflection

Given more time, I would have:

  • Met CAPSEA employees in-person to thoroughly understand their day-to-day and map out interdependent tasks.

  • Taken time to go step-by-step through CAPSEA’s call process and relationship between departments to see common use cases.

  • Understand the perspective of individuals seeking assistance (due to privacy and confidentiality reasons, we did not seek to speak with those individuals during our project).

  • Quantified the impact of digitized, prompt, and accurate data collection for the organization at a high level and at the individual level.

Outcomes

From this project, I learned the following:

  • Small efficiencies—minimizing time to communicate and share data—are compounded when used across an entire organization.

  • Motivation at the organizational level and at the individual level are needed to help bring about change.

  • Communication with stakeholders in the perspective of their role helps provide a clear, detailed picture of their interactions and focus day-to-day.
